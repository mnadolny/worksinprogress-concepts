## fig-02-fort-worth.png

- **Caption:** The edge of Fort Worth, Texas. There are no public transport networks and few walkable amenities for new subdivisions to tie into, making it difficult for planners to avoid low densities and heavy parking provision. This illustrates how car-dependent low-density urbanism tends to self-perpetuate.
- **Source:** Apple Maps
- **Original filename:** Screenshot-2026-05-08-at-6.31.55-PM.png
- **Position in article:** Section "Spanish cities today" — after the opening paragraph of the section

## fig-05-buckingham.png

- **Caption:** This image shows the English town of Buckingham, which has the common developer-led pattern of urbanism, in which the authorities develop a few key arterial roads and then allow developers to tap into them for fragmented housing developments. This example is particularly bad, but basically representative of late-20th century planning in both Britain and the United States.
- **Source:** Apple Maps
- **Original filename:** Screenshot-2026-05-15-at-11.54.01-AM.png
- **Position in article:** Section "Spanish cities today" — after the paragraph on Spain being distinctive in Southern Europe, before the comparison of British/American vs Spanish planning systems
